FOUNDER SPEECH

Children who are living and working in the streets and are inadequately protected, supervised, or directed by responsible adults of our society. The first thing here we mentioned that the term “street children” is considered very disrespectful and makes the vulnerable youth feel like they have absolutely no value, as do street dogs. Instead, the appropriate term we try to use when referring to this community is.....“children living on the streets of India” The children’s earnings must be spent risk being robbed if they keep cash on them overnight. With such a short time-frame to turn over their begging funds they often spend money on entertainment, such as seeing the latest movies. They’ve been exposed to gambling, drinking, drugs, and prostitution. Children who are living on the streets battle addiction to local alcohol and sniffing inhalants such as sniffing dendrite, glue, and nail polish. Some suffer from paid interactions with sex workers, who are often girls who’ve run away from home. Small percentage of these children are trying to support their siblings or other homeless children through the funds they’ve earned on the street. Children who run away from home, after being repeatedly abused by their parents or relatives. Abuse is a common reason why children run away from home in India. A child arrives alone on a train platform in India every five minutes. Most of these children are in need of protection and care, Don’t let them use your phone, in order to avoid it being stolen — how to handle the situation. It is important to remember that most of these children lack of respectful interactions with other humans.The most important thing is to treat them with respect as you would to any other person and show them some decency. These children feel invisible and abandoned by their government and the adults who were supposed to care for them. Don’t expect anything in return, though, “What is very important is for the giver to give with a kind heart and clear vision,” K. Jaiswal President TEARS

Education And Training

Capacity building and knowledge sharing are processes that can further result in an increased capacity of personnel working in the field of street children to recognize the value of an evidence-based approach. Minimum standards should be set for all personnel and everyone who intends to be engaged in working with street children. Everyone should be accountable for the child ethical code of conduct. Furthermore, the state should ensure that all duty bearers have the capacity to carry out their obligations. In other words, states should develop specific capacity building and training interventions for caregivers, teachers, social workers, law enforcement officers, doctors, and others responsible for street children’s wellbeing.

The implementation of a program for street children requires the work of various specialists, such as facilitators (social worker, teacher, psychologist, medical staff, cook, and guard) and program support staff (manager/coordinator/assistant). Each staff member should be provided with continuous training on street children’s issues: their lifestyles, behavior, attitudes to others, and matters of daily survival. Service providers should be allowed to train staff and continuously increase their professionalism. Trainings are usually offered by relevant NGOs who are responsible for service provision as well as monitoring that the knowledge gained during the trainings is properly applied to the daily routine.
